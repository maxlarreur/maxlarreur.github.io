Nine-banded armadillo spatiotemporal behavior near the northern edge of their expanding distribution

Nine-banded armadillos (Dasypus novemcinctus) have expanded their range northward and eastward and have behaviorally plastic diurnal activity patterns. However, landscape features influencing their distribution and activity are generally unknown in recently colonized areas. We used occupancy modeling and activity analysis to investigate spatiotemporal patterns of armadillos at multiple spatial scales in newly colonized areas near the leading edge of their northerly range in southern Illinois. Expansion of armadillos may continue as land use practices continue to create seminatural refugia and dispersal corridors in forested areas along roadways.

The precipitous decline of a gray fox population

This chapter focuses on the gray fox population throughout southern Illinois. We use independent data collected from two larger-scale camera trapping projects conducted throughout the southern 16,000 km2 of Illinois. Using these independent datasets, we develop a "past", "expected", and "contemporary" model for gray fox occupancy to identify potential changes in their space use and distribution. Unfortunately, our results indicated a dramatic decline in gray fox occupancy over a decadal time period, with decreased occupancy in preferred forest habitats.

Decadal temporal dynamics and potential niche separation amongst carnivores

Using two independent datasets collected from larger-scale camera trapping projects conducted throughout the southern 16,000 km2 of Illinois, we quantify behavioral stability and changes for native and domestic carnivores. Native species include coyote (Canis latrans), bobcat (Lynx rufus), red fox (Vulpes vulpes), gray fox (Urocyon cinereoargenteus), striped skunk (Mephitis mephitis), and raccoon (Procyon lotor).
